<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="CCl4 study design">
    <div>Twenty percent CCl4 in olive oil was injected intraperitoneally 3 times per week for 10 weeks.</div>
    <div>Efocipegtrutide was administered subcutaneously during the last 6 weeks.</div>
    <div>Liver tissue samples were prepared after treatment and evaluated with Sirius red staining.</div>
    <div>Hepatic hydroxyproline and pro-collagen 1 alpha 1 were quantified.</div>
    <div>qRT-PCR and blood collection were used to evaluate fibrosis and inflammation surrogate markers.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Model Rationale</h3>
      <p>The source describes CCl4-induced fibrosis as a well-established model of liver fibrosis caused by hepatocyte damage, necrosis, inflammation, and fibrosis.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Preclinical Context</h3>
      <p>The poster places this CCl4 study alongside prior efocipegtrutide work in MCD, AMLN, CDAHFD, TAA, and bile duct ligation models.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Efocipegtrutide improved liver fibrosis in CCl4-induced fibrosis mice.</li>
    <li>The source says the CCl4 findings were consistent with previous efocipegtrutide results in NASH and fibrosis animal models.</li>
    <li>The poster attributes the anti-fibrotic effect to simultaneous action of glucagon, GIP, and GLP-1.</li>
    <li>The source states that human study was ongoing to assess clinical relevance of the non-clinical findings.</li>
  </ul>
