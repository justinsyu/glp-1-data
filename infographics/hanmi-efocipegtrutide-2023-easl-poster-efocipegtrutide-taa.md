<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="TAA study design">
    <div>TAA was injected intraperitoneally for 12 weeks to induce liver injury and fibrosis in mice.</div>
    <div>Efocipegtrutide, formerly HM15211, was administered during the last 10 weeks.</div>
    <div>Semaglutide and tirzepatide were included as comparative controls.</div>
    <div>Dosing levels were based on human relevant dosage used in NASH clinical studies.</div>
    <div>Readouts included Sirius red staining, H&amp;E staining, hydroxyproline, qPCR, ELISA, and histologic grading.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Therapeutic Rationale</h3>
      <p>The poster describes efocipegtrutide as a long-acting glucagon/GIP/GLP-1 triple agonist conjugated with human IgG<sub>4</sub> Fc fragment via a short PEG linker.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Comparator Context</h3>
      <p>The source compared efocipegtrutide with semaglutide and tirzepatide in the TAA model, using fibrosis, inflammation, and surrogate marker readouts.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>Efocipegtrutide improved liver inflammation and fibrosis in TAA mice.</li>
    <li>The source reports greater improvement over semaglutide and tirzepatide in fibrosis and portal inflammation scores.</li>
    <li>Surrogate marker readouts were consistent with histologic improvements, including reductions in TGF-beta, Col1a1, and serum TIMP-1.</li>
    <li>The poster states that these results rationalize an ongoing phase 2b study in biopsy-proven NASH subjects.</li>
