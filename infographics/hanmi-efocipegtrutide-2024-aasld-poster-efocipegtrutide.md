<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="TAA study design">
    <div>TAA was injected intraperitoneally into mice for 12 weeks to induce liver injury and fibrosis.</div>
    <div>Efocipegtrutide was administered during the final 10 weeks of the model.</div>
    <div>Tirzepatide, a GLP-1/GIP dual agonist, was included as a comparative control.</div>
    <div>Efpegerglucagon, described as having the same structure as efocipegtrutide but only GCG activity, was included.</div>
    <div>Readouts included hydroxyproline, pro-collagen 1 alpha 1, Sirius Red, H&amp;E, marker gene expression, TIMP-1, and histological grading.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Mechanistic Question</h3>
      <p>The source tests whether the glucagon action of efocipegtrutide contributes to anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic effects in the TAA model.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Comparator Logic</h3>
      <p>The poster compares efocipegtrutide with tirzepatide and efpegerglucagon to separate triple agonism from GLP-1/GIP activity and glucagon engagement.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>In TAA mice, efocipegtrutide and efpegerglucagon showed similar anti-inflammatory and anti-fibrotic benefits.</li>
    <li>Tirzepatide had relatively little effect in the biochemical, histological, and marker readouts shown in the source.</li>
    <li>The source concludes that glucagon engagement could play a crucial role in managing hepatic inflammation and fibrosis.</li>
    <li>The poster states that a phase 2b study in biopsy confirmed MASH patients, NCT04505436, was ongoing to assess clinical relevance.</li>
  </ul>
