<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="Study design">
    <div>Five-week-old female Sprague Dawley rats were fed 60% kcal fat diet for 8 weeks.</div>
    <div>Diet-induced obese osteoporosis model was induced by surgical oophorectomy, or OVX.</div>
    <div>Animals received vehicle, liraglutide 25 nmol/kg BID, or HM15211 2.2 or 4.4 nmol/kg Q2D.</div>
    <div>Serum bone markers were measured on days 14 and 28.</div>
    <div>Femur BMD was monitored with high-resolution in vivo u-CT.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Bone Marker Panel</h3>
      <p>The source measured Glu-OC as a bone resorption marker, OPG as an osteoclastogenesis inhibition marker, and P1NP as a bone formation marker.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Cell Mechanism Work</h3>
      <p>MC3T3-E1 mouse osteoblast cells were used to evaluate osteoblast differentiation markers, collagen protein expression, and anti-apoptotic activity.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>In the obese-osteoporosis rat model, HM15211 lowered serum Glu-OC and increased OPG and P1NP compared with vehicle and liraglutide groups.</li>
    <li>HM15211 showed comparable femur BMD with vehicle while producing greater weight loss than liraglutide.</li>
    <li>In osteoblast cells, HM15211 increased collagen and Gla-OC expression, and the source states that these effects were blunted by GIPR-mediated signaling inhibition.</li>
    <li>The poster concludes that HM15211 might provide potent weight loss without bone loss.</li>
  </ul>
