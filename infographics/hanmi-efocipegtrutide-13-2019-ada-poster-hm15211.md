<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="2019 ADA study design">
    <div>MCD-diet mice were used for NASH and fibrosis studies after 6 or 12 weeks induction.</div>
    <div>After 4 to 5 weeks of HM15211 treatment, liver tissue was analyzed for TG, TBARS, inflammatory markers, HSC activation markers, and fibrosis markers.</div>
    <div>MRI analysis was performed every 2 weeks to monitor hepatic lipid content noninvasively.</div>
    <div>Biopsy-proven obese, NASH, and fibrosis monkeys had BMI greater than 40 kg/m2 and NAS plus fibrosis score greater than 7.</div>
    <div>Monkey readouts included body weight, blood lipid profiles, liver MRI-PDFF, and liver biopsy histology.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Therapeutic Rationale</h3>
      <p>The poster states that liver fat accumulation and inflammation are associated with NASH progression, and that targeting both aspects may contribute to NASH resolution and fibrosis improvement.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Model Scope</h3>
      <p>The source evaluates HM15211 in MCD-diet mice and in obese/NASH nonhuman primates.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>HM15211 is designed to treat NASH and fibrosis by targeting both steatosis and hepatic inflammation.</li>
    <li>In MCD-diet mice, HM15211 reduced liver fat and inflammation and improved fibrosis regardless of fibrosis stage.</li>
    <li>The source states that beneficial effects on NASH and fibrosis improvement were reproduced in obese/NASH nonhuman primates.</li>
    <li>The poster concludes that HM15211 might be a novel therapeutic option for NASH and fibrosis, with further studies needed to assess clinical relevance.</li>
  </ul>
