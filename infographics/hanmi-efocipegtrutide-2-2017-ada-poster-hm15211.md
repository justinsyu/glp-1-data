<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="2017 poster study schema">
    <div>In vitro assays tested cAMP accumulation in CHO cells expressing human GLP-1R, GCGR, or GIPR.</div>
    <div>DIO mouse studies evaluated body weight loss, food intake, cholesterol, and energy expenditure after 4 weeks of treatment.</div>
    <div>Normal C57BL/6 mice were used for ipGTT to evaluate hyperglycemic risk in the presence or absence of GLP-1 and GIP antagonists.</div>
    <div>MCD mice were used as a NASH model to measure hepatic triglycerides, TBARS, lobular inflammation, and NAFLD activity score.</div>
    <div>HSD rats were used to test body weight change, blood cholesterol, and hepatic steatosis.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Triple-Agonist Profile</h3>
      <p>The source states that HM15211 has relatively high glucagon activity and balanced GLP-1/GIP activity, reproduced across batches.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Metabolic Rationale</h3>
      <p>The abstract attributes potent body weight loss to glucagon activity while stating that GLP-1 and GIP agonism could balance glucagon-induced glucose production.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>The poster concludes that HM15211 is a long-acting triple agonist with high glucagon and balanced GLP-1/GIP action.</li>
    <li>In DIO mice, HM15211 showed greater body weight loss than liraglutide in both QW mimic and QM mimic studies.</li>
    <li>The source links HM15211 body weight effects to increased energy expenditure, not only food intake inhibition.</li>
    <li>In MCD mice and HSD rats, HM15211 improved NASH-related readouts including hepatic TG, TBARS, NAS, cholesterol, and steatosis.</li>
  </ul>
