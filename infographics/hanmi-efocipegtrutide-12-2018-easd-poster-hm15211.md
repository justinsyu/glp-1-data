<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="Study design">
    <div>In vitro assays tested whether HM15211 changed HM12460A human insulin receptor phosphorylation potency.</div>
    <div>Reciprocal in vitro assays tested whether HM12460A changed HM15211 activity at human GLP-1R, GCGR, or GIPR.</div>
    <div>db/db mice were used as a genetic diabetes model with obesity and type 2 diabetes phenotype.</div>
    <div>DIO/STZ rats were used as an acquired diabetes model combining high-fat diet insulin resistance and partial beta-cell destruction.</div>
    <div>In vivo readouts included blood glucose, HbA1c, and body weight change.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Poster Hypothesis</h3>
      <p>The source hypothesized that HM15211 could maximize the exogenous insulin response through body weight loss and subsequent insulin sensitivity improvement.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Animal Dosing Context</h3>
      <p>The source frames the combination as a weekly insulin and triple agonist combo, while the animal studies used Q2D dosing in db/db mice and Q3D dosing in DIO/STZ rats.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>In diabetic animal models, basal insulin and triple agonist combo provided better glycemic control versus insulin alone.</li>
    <li>The combo produced more weight loss than insulin alone and than the insulin and GLP-1RA comparator combo in db/db mice.</li>
    <li>The source positions a triple agonist as a potential additional combo partner for basal insulin.</li>
    <li>The poster emphasizes improved glycemic control and body weight loss as the main therapeutic potential signals.</li>
