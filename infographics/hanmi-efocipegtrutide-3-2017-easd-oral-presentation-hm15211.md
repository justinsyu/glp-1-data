<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="2017 EASD oral presentation study map">
    <div>HM15211 is described as a long-acting GLP-1/GIP/glucagon tri-agonist conjugated to human IgG Fc through a flexible linker.</div>
    <div>The objective was to assess efficacy and related modes of action in NASH and obesity animal models.</div>
    <div>DIO mice were used for hepatic lipid gene expression, serum lipids, body weight change, energy expenditure, RER, and hepatic TG.</div>
    <div>MCD mice were treated for 4 weeks to evaluate hepatic TG, TBARS, ALT, bilirubin, inflammatory markers, NAS, and fibrosis markers.</div>
    <div>DIO rats and pair-fed DIO mice were used to test body weight effects and the food-intake-independent component of weight loss.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Profile Rationale</h3>
      <p>The source describes high glucagon activity as suitable for liver-preferential distribution and NASH treatment, with balanced GLP-1 and GIP action to neutralize hyperglycemic risk.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>PK And Distribution Context</h3>
      <p>The source reports half-life ranges of 42.7 to 55 hours in mice and 82.8 to 85.7 hours in rats, and liver tissue-to-serum ratio of 50.6% at 48 hours in SD rats.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>HM15211 is described as a novel long-acting tri-agonist with high glucagon and balanced GLP-1 and GIP activity.</li>
    <li>In NASH animal models, the source reports favorable hepatic lipid gene reprogramming, improved lipid profiles, lower hepatic TG and oxidative stress, reduced NAS, and reduced fibrogenic markers.</li>
    <li>In obese animal models, HM15211 produced greater body weight loss than daily GLP-1RA comparators in the reported DIO mouse and rat studies.</li>
    <li>The source concludes that HM15211 might provide favorable therapeutic efficacy for NASH and obesity versus existing therapeutics.</li>
  </ul>
