<section>
  <h2>Study At A Glance</h2>
  <div class="flow-diagram" aria-label="Dyslipidemia study design">
    <div>High-fat and high-fructose fed hamsters were used to evaluate dyslipidemia efficacy.</div>
    <div>Comparators included evolocumab and rosuvastatin in the hamster cholesterol study.</div>
    <div>Normal mice were given olive oil in an oral lipid tolerance test, followed by blood TG monitoring.</div>
    <div>HepG2 cells and hamster liver tissue were used for LDLR, HMGCR, LDL uptake, and gene expression readouts.</div>
    <div>The source frames HM15211 as a GLP-1/GIP/glucagon triple agonist that could affect multiple lipid metabolism pathways.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="evidence-grid evidence-grid-2">
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Therapeutic Rationale</h3>
      <p>The poster states that current dyslipidemia drugs have limited efficacy when acting through a single target, while HM15211 was expected to affect multiple lipid metabolism pathways.</p>
    </article>
    <article class="evidence-card">
      <h3>Mechanistic Scope</h3>
      <p>The source tested lipid absorption, LDL clearance, cholesterol synthesis, and free fatty acid metabolism as possible mechanisms for cholesterol lowering.</p>
    </article>
  </div>
</section>

<section>
  <h2>Source Conclusions</h2>
  <ul>
    <li>In dyslipidemia hamsters, HM15211 produced greater cholesterol lowering than the commercial dyslipidemia comparators shown in the source.</li>
    <li>The poster identifies four responsible mechanisms: inhibition of lipid absorption, enhanced LDL clearance, inhibition of cholesterol synthesis, and improved free fatty acid metabolism.</li>
    <li>The source concludes that HM15211 might be a therapeutic option for dyslipidemia patients.</li>
  </ul>
